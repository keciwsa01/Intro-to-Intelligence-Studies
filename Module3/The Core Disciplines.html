The Core Disciplines

How Intelligence is Gathered (INTs)

Overview of Collection Disciplines (INTs)

Different methods of intelligence collection, each with unique strengths and weaknesses
A

Geospatial Intelligence (GEOINT)

Imagery and geospatial data analysis

What it Is

Derived from imagery and geospatial data, describing features and activities and their locations on, above, or below Earth's surface. Managed by the National Geospatial-Intelligence Agency (NGA).
Historical Evolution
  • From French army balloon photos (1794) to planes, and later satellites
  • Corona Program: First operational spy satellite (late 1950s), developed as U-2 planes had limited life
  • Used film-based cameras with improving resolution (from ~40 feet to ~1 foot) and required capsule recovery by aircraft

Types of Imagery

  • Electro-optical (EO): Standard visual imagery
  • Radar (SAR): Synthetic Aperture Radar
  • Infrared (IR): Heat signature detection
  • Multispectral/Hyperspectral (MSI/HSI): Multiple wavelength analysis
Modern Tools
  • UAVs (Drones): Offer real-time video and can loiter over targets, addressing the snapshot problem
  • Commercial Imagery: Frees classified systems, used for mapping, disaster response (e.g., WorldView-1)
Strengths
  • Clear visuals, easy for policymakers to understand ("a picture is worth a thousand words")
  • Self-revealing (e.g., troop exercises)
  • Widely compelling evidence
Weaknesses
  • "Snapshot problem" (where & when, not why)
  • Requires expert photo interpreters
  • Susceptible to deception (camouflage, decoys)
  • Risk of over-reliance
B

Signals Intelligence (SIGINT)

Intercepted communications and electronic signals

What it Is

Information collected by intercepting signals (radio, phone, satellite transmissions, electronic emissions). The NSA is a key collector.

Types

  • COMINT: Communications intelligence
  • TELINT: Telemetry intelligence from weapons tests
  • ELINT/FISINT: Electronic emissions from radars

Legal and Ethical Dilemmas

Post-9/11 Changes
  • Expanded surveillance post-9/11 (PATRIOT Act)
  • Controversies over bulk metadata collection (Snowden leaks)
  • Subsequent reforms (USA FREEDOM Act)
Strengths
  • Can show intentions and plans, not just capabilities
  • Offers early warning capabilities
Weaknesses
  • If enemies use secure communications or encryption, intercepts dry up ("going dark")
  • Flood of modern communications overwhelms analysts
  • Terrorist groups adapt with couriers or single-use phones
C

Measurement and Signatures Intelligence (MASINT)

The "science and forensics branch"

What it Is

The "science and forensics branch," looking at physical signatures like vibrations, heat, radiation, chemical traces, or unusual radar reflections.

Six Disciplines

  • Electro-optical
  • Geophysical
  • Materials
  • Nuclear radiation
  • Radar
  • Radio frequency

Key Contributions

  • Detects WMD development
  • Monitors environmental issues
  • Tracks weapons development and deception
  • Supports arms control and treaty verification
Strengths
  • Reveals hidden or disguised activities
  • Complements other INTs for powerful fusion
Weaknesses
  • Highly technical, less understood by policymakers
  • Requires specialized analysts
  • Currently shared between the DIA and NGA, not an independent agency
D

Human Intelligence (HUMINT)

Intelligence collected from people

What it Is

Intelligence collected from people (espionage, clandestine collection, overt diplomatic reporting), often involving face-to-face recruitment and risk. Primarily the CIA Directorate of Operations (DO).
Agent Acquisition Cycle
  • Targeting
  • Assessing
  • Recruiting
  • Handling
  • Termination

Challenges in the Digital Age

Modern Complications
  • Social media footprints make cover more difficult
  • Counterintelligence threats have increased
  • Digital surveillance makes traditional tradecraft harder
Strengths
  • Provides unique access to plans, intentions, and thoughts that technical INTs cannot
  • Relatively cheap compared to satellites
Weaknesses
  • Risky (lives and diplomatic fallout)
  • Fragile and slow to develop
  • Vulnerable to deception (double agents, dangles)
E

Open-Source Intelligence (OSINT)

Intelligence from publicly available information

What it Is

Intelligence built from publicly available information (news, social media, government reports, academic work).
Became critical during the COVID-19 pandemic when other collection methods were hampered
Advantages
  • Readily accessible and cheap
  • Provides crucial context (the "edges of the puzzle")
  • No legal or ethical barriers to collection
Disadvantages / Challenges
  • Volume overload (the "wheat vs. chaff" problem)
  • Bias and reliability issues
  • Fake news and AI manipulation
  • Often undervalued culturally within the intelligence community
